## Data Review  - Jed Crocker


1. Selected Data Sources

Definite:

- US Census 2000 and 2010

- Population Projections


Under consideration / hope to explore further:

  • Risk Layers
  • Storm & Climate Data
  • Demographics


US Census 2000 and 2010

This dataset appears to be rich with many layers of information, which is something I would expect from a census survey. As the data is described in the general metadata file: “Both of the Shapefiles with block polygons have 100 plus demographic (both population and housing) variables defined by US census to collect data.” I am interested in working with this dataset due to its granularity of information and many potential variables that could potentially be explored (if I am understanding the description correctly!) I am further interested in this dataset due to the terms included in the description: Shapefiles, what are they? How do I work with them? The same goes for block polygons. I have been able to load the refined geojson files into QGIS but that has been the extent of my exploration thus far. 


Population Projections

This dataset stands out among the other Urban Ecology Lab data collections due to its straightforward name and format (excel, tables within tabs). Beyond that, though -- I think this data could be a very useful pairing when combined with available datasets at our disposal. It seems feasible that a meaningful connection could be made to much of this available data (risk layers, storm & climate data, land cover, temperature, green infrastructure) when viewing through the lens of proportion of growth that can be determined from the Population Projections dataset. There is a useful granularity to this dataset, as well -- population growth can be projected by NYC borough, by age, by male vs female, by certain age groupings. 


Risk Layers

This dataset is appealing due to its potential various points of entry -- the ‘DifferentRisks’ folder contains file sets for heat risk, coastal risk, and inland risk. (I have yet to determine if these three folders are interrelated or function individually.) Another point of entry that caught my eye was the risk_pm2p5 folder -- although the contents of this folder only include .adf files, I thought perhaps the p5 on the end of this folder title was a reference that this portion of the data might be suited for working within p5.js. (No p5.js sketch folder exists in this data, as far as I can tell.)  I understand that .adf files are rastergrid files that I can view through QGIS. It would be interesting to examine these risk layers individually or in combination with the census / population projection data referenced above.


Storm & Climate Data

I am interested in working with this dataset because it appears to be extremely robust. The data comes with a pdf of definitions from the source (GHCN) and there appears to be more documentation online. I am equally nervous of working with this dataset because that data itself seems very complex! That is why it is being placed in the ‘pending decision / would like to explore further’ list, when considering my first qualitative project. However, it seems to me that with so many available fields within the data, there could be a lot of room to build interesting visualizations. 


Demographics

This information looks appealing similar to the above Storm & Climate dataset --- there is a pdf key with definitions for each of the variables, and the data is described as very granular. With so much information available, in many fields, it seems likely that there will be a lot of space to explore and learn while attempting to create visual representations of the data contained within.
